How to Use This Drywall Calculator

Enter your room's length, width, and wall height. Choose whether to cover walls, ceiling, or both. Add any doors or windows so their area gets subtracted from the total. Adjust the waste slider, pick your sheet size, and hit Calculate Drywall. You'll instantly see how many sheets to buy, the total square footage, and an estimated material cost.

Why This Matters

Buying the wrong amount of drywall is one of the most common (and costly) mistakes in home renovation. Under-buy and you'll make a second trip to the hardware store mid-project — delays, mismatched lot numbers, extra labor hours. Over-buy and you're stuck storing bulky 4×8 sheets or absorbing a return fee.

For a typical 12×12 room with 8-foot ceilings, you need roughly 384 sq ft of wall area (minus doors and windows) plus 144 sq ft of ceiling — that's around 17–20 sheets of 4×8 drywall before waste. Add 10–15% for cuts around outlets, corners, and doorframes, and that number climbs to 19–23 sheets. Professional drywalling crews always over-order by at least 10% for this exact reason.

Whether you're a weekend DIYer tackling a basement finish or a contractor bidding a multi-room job, accurate sheet counts keep your budget on track from day one.

How It's Calculated

The calculator follows these steps:

1. Total wall area = 2 × (Length + Width) × Wall Height

2. Subtract openings = Total wall area − Σ(door width × door height) − Σ(window width × window height)

3. Add ceiling (optional) = Length × Width

4. Net area = Wall area (net) + Ceiling area

5. Raw sheets = Net area ÷ Sheet area (always rounded up)

6. Sheets with waste = Raw sheets × (1 + Waste% ÷ 100), rounded up to nearest whole sheet

7. Cost = Sheets with waste × Price per sheet

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Always round up — you can't buy half a sheet. Every partial sheet counts as one full sheet.
  • Don't skip the waste factor — even a simple rectangular room requires cuts at every corner, window, and outlet box. 10% is the bare minimum; use 15% for L-shaped rooms or vaulted ceilings.
  • Match sheet height to ceiling height — 4×12 sheets are standard for 9-ft ceilings and reduce horizontal seams significantly, cutting taping time.
  • Subtract only 50% of openings if doors are wide — some pros keep the door area in to account for the extra cuts and strips needed around the frame. This calculator subtracts 100%; add a few extra sheets manually if in doubt.
  • Check panel thickness — 1/2" is standard for walls; 5/8" Type X is required for fire-rated assemblies (garage walls, stairwells). This doesn't affect sheet count but does affect weight and price.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much drywall do I need for a 12×12 room?

A 12×12 room with 8-ft ceilings has about 384 sq ft of wall area. Subtract one standard door (21 sq ft) and one window (12 sq ft) = ~351 sq ft net. With a 10% waste factor, you need roughly 13 sheets of 4×8 for walls alone. Add the 144 sq ft ceiling and that's ~18 total sheets.

Should I use 4×8 or 4×12 sheets?

4×12 sheets create fewer seams and are preferred for rooms with 9–10 ft ceilings. They're heavier and harder to maneuver solo, but professional finishers favor them because less taping = less finishing labor. For 8-ft ceilings, 4×8 is the most common choice.

What waste factor should I use?

10% is the standard for simple rectangular rooms. Use 15% for rooms with multiple corners, angled ceilings, archways, or lots of windows. For curved walls or complex geometry, go 20%+. It's better to have two extra sheets than to run short halfway through taping.

Does this calculator account for double-layer drywall?

Not automatically. For double-layer applications (common in soundproofing or fire-rated assemblies), simply multiply the total sheets by 2. Run the calculator once and double the sheet count and cost manually.
