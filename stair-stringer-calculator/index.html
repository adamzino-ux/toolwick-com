How to Use This Stair Stringer Calculator

Enter your total rise (floor-to-floor vertical height), your desired riser height, and your preferred tread depth. The calculator will automatically find the optimal number of steps, recalculate the exact riser height to fit evenly, and output the stringer length, angle, and a complete cut list for each step.

Why This Matters

Getting stair stringers right is one of the most critical parts of any deck, basement, or exterior stair project. A miscalculation of even half an inch per step compounds across every tread — resulting in stairs that feel off, fail inspection, or worse, become a safety hazard.

Consider a common scenario: you're building a deck that sits 84 inches off the ground. With a target 7" riser, you'd calculate 12 steps. But if you ordered lumber based on rough math and cut it wrong, you could end up with an uneven bottom or top step — a code violation in most jurisdictions. Most building codes (IRC 2021) require risers between 4" and 7¾" and treads at least 10" deep. Stringer boards must also maintain a minimum 3.5" net section after notching to preserve structural integrity.

This calculator also shows the notch depth — how deep you're cutting into the stringer — so you can verify you're not weakening the board. Choosing 2×12 lumber over 2×10 gives you crucial extra margin when notch depth is tight.

How It's Calculated

Number of Risers = Round(Total Rise ÷ Desired Riser Height)
Actual Riser = Total Rise ÷ Number of Risers
Total Run = (Number of Risers − 1) × Tread Depth
Stringer Length = √(Total Rise² + Total Run²)
Angle = arctan(Total Rise ÷ Total Run) in degrees
Notch Depth = √(Actual Riser² + Tread Depth²) − Board Width + small margin

The number of steps equals the number of risers. The top landing is the floor itself, so the total run uses risers − 1 treads. The stringer length is the hypotenuse of the right triangle formed by total rise and total run, plus a small overhang margin. The notch depth check ensures you have at least 3.5" of solid wood remaining in the stringer after each cut.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Measure total rise accurately. Measure from finished floor to finished floor — not subfloor to subfloor. Tread thickness changes your effective rise at the bottom step.
  • Account for tread thickness at the bottom. The bottom riser is typically shorter by one tread thickness so every step feels the same height when walking up.
  • Don't cut too deep into stringers. Over-notching weakens the board structurally. If notch depth exceeds 1/3 of the board width, use a wider board or a different rise/run combination.
  • Check the angle. A comfortable stair angle is typically 30°–37°. Steeper than 45° is uncomfortable and may not be code-compliant for occupied buildings.
  • Order 10–15% extra lumber. Cutting errors happen, and lumber lengths come in standard foot increments. Round up to the nearest even foot.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the ideal rise and run for stairs?

Most building codes specify a maximum riser height of 7¾" and a minimum tread depth of 10". A comfortable rule of thumb is that rise + run should equal approximately 17–18 inches. For example, a 7" rise with an 11" run sums to 18" — a very comfortable stair.

How many stringers do I need for a staircase?

For stairs up to 36" wide, 3 stringers (two outer, one center) are standard. Wider stairs need additional stringers to prevent tread flex. Most codes require stringers no more than 16" apart on center for 2× lumber treads.

What size lumber should I use for stair stringers?

2×12 dimensional lumber (actual size 1.5" × 11.25") is the most common choice because it leaves the most wood after notching. 2×10 works for smaller rise/run combinations, but you should always verify that at least 3.5" of solid wood remains after the largest notch cut.

Why does my bottom step feel different than the rest?

This is almost always because the tread thickness was not subtracted from the bottom riser height. The bottom riser should be reduced by one tread thickness (e.g., 1.5" for 5/4 deck boards) so that the vertical distance from finished floor to finished tread surface matches every other step.
