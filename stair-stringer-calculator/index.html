Why This Matters

Stair stringers are the diagonal backbone of any staircase, and even a small miscalculation can create stairs that are uncomfortable, unsafe, or fail building code inspection. The IRC (International Residential Code) requires riser heights between 4" and 7¾" and tread depths of at least 10". If your risers vary by more than ⅜" from one step to the next, you fail inspection — and someone can trip.

Consider a deck with a total rise of 108" (9 feet). Divide by 9 steps and you get a 12" riser — way too tall. Divide by 14 steps and you get 7.71" — just barely compliant. This calculator does that math instantly and flags violations. Builders, remodelers, and serious DIYers use this tool before they ever pick up a saw, saving hours of rework and expensive lumber waste on a typical project costing $400–$2,000 in materials alone.

How It's Calculated

The core formula is straightforward:

  • Riser Height = Total Rise ÷ Number of Risers
  • Number of Risers = ⌈Total Rise ÷ 7⌉ (aiming for ≤7" per riser)
  • Total Run = (Number of Risers − 1) × Tread Depth (treads = risers − 1)
  • Stringer Length = √(Total Rise² + Total Run²) — the hypotenuse of the rise/run triangle
  • Plumb Cut Angle = arctan(Rise ÷ Run) — angle of the vertical cuts
  • Level Cut Angle = 90° − Plumb Cut Angle
  • Minimum Board Width = Riser Height + Tread Depth (to ensure enough material remains after cuts)

The kicker plate thickness is subtracted from the first riser height to keep all steps equal after the bottom plate is installed.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Measure twice, cut once: Always measure total rise from finished floor to finished floor — not subfloor to subfloor. Adding flooring material later changes your riser height.
  • The 18" rule: Riser + Tread should equal 17"–18" for comfortable walking. A 7" riser with an 11" tread = 18" — perfect.
  • Don't ignore the kicker plate: The bottom riser must be reduced by the thickness of your bottom plate (typically 1.5") or your bottom step will be taller than all others.
  • Board width matters: After cutting notches for each step, you must have at least 3.5" of wood remaining on the stringer for structural integrity.
  • Code compliance first: Always check local building codes — some jurisdictions are stricter than the IRC minimums, especially for commercial or public stairs.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a stair stringer?
A stair stringer is the diagonal structural board (usually a 2×12) that runs along each side (and sometimes center) of a staircase, supporting the treads and risers. It's notched in a zigzag pattern — each notch receives one tread (horizontal surface) and one riser (vertical face). Most residential staircases use 3 stringers.
How many risers do I need?
Divide your total rise by your desired riser height (typically 7") and round up to the nearest whole number. For example, a 91" total rise ÷ 7" = 13 risers. Note that the number of treads is always one less than the number of risers, because the top landing counts as the final "tread."
What lumber size should I use for stringers?
2×12 dimensional lumber (actual: 1.5" × 11.25") is the standard for stair stringers. After notching for treads and risers, you need at least 3.5" of solid wood remaining — called the "effective depth." For very steep or wide stairs, engineered lumber (LVL) may be required.
What is the "comfortable" stair formula?
The traditional comfort formula is: Riser + Tread = 17" to 18", or alternatively 2 × Riser + Tread = 24" to 25". A 7" riser with a 10" tread sums to 17" — comfortably within the comfortable range and meets IRC code minimums. Steeper stairs (higher riser, shallower tread) are tiring; shallower stairs use more floor space.
