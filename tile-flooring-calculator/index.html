How to Use This Tile Calculator

Enter your room's length and width (or switch to "Known Area" if you already have the square footage), then enter your tile dimensions. Adjust the waste percentage slider to add a safety buffer for cuts and breakage. Optionally, add tiles per box and pricing to get a full cost estimate. Hit Calculate and you'll instantly see how many tiles to buy.

Why This Matters

Buying the wrong amount of tile is one of the most common — and expensive — DIY mistakes. Buy too few and you'll face a second trip to the store, only to find your tile color or batch has been discontinued. Buy too many and you've wasted money on materials that sit in your garage.

Consider a typical bathroom remodel: a 9 ft × 7 ft bathroom (63 sq ft) using 12"×12" tiles. Without accounting for waste, you'd need exactly 63 tiles. But once you factor in cuts around the toilet, vanity, and walls — typically 10–15% waste — you actually need 70–73 tiles. If those tiles come in boxes of 10, that means 8 boxes instead of 7.

Professional tilers routinely recommend a 10% waste factor for straightforward layouts, 15% for diagonal patterns, and up to 20–25% for complex designs or natural stone that requires matching. This calculator lets you dial in exactly the right buffer for your project.

How It's Calculated

The calculator converts all measurements to a common unit (square feet), then applies this logic:

Room Area (sq ft) = Length × Width Tile Area (sq ft) = (Tile Length + Grout Gap) × (Tile Width + Grout Gap) ÷ 144 Base Tiles = CEILING(Room Area ÷ Tile Area) Total Tiles = CEILING(Base Tiles × (1 + Waste% ÷ 100)) Boxes Needed = CEILING(Total Tiles ÷ Tiles per Box) Total Cost = Boxes × Price per Box (or Tiles × Price per Tile)

The CEILING function rounds up to the nearest whole number — you can't buy a fraction of a tile. Grout gap is added to each tile dimension so the effective tile spacing is accounted for in the coverage calculation.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Always buy 10% extra minimum. Even for simple square rooms, cuts at edges mean waste. For diagonal layouts, use 15–20%.
  • Save a few tiles from each box. Store leftover tiles in case you need a repair years later — dye lots change, and an exact match may be impossible to find.
  • Measure twice. Don't forget to subtract areas that won't be tiled, like kitchen islands, large cabinets, or bathtubs.
  • Check tile batch numbers. When buying, ensure all boxes are from the same production batch (look for "LOT" or "SHADE" numbers) to avoid subtle color variations.
  • Include the grout gap. A 1/8" grout line on a 12"×12" tile effectively makes each tile take up ~12.125"×12.125" of space. Over 100 tiles, this adds up.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much waste percentage should I use?

For a simple rectangular room with straight-set tiles, 10% is standard. Use 15% for diagonal (45°) patterns, and 20–25% for herringbone, chevron, or natural stone. If your room has many obstacles like columns or alcoves, round up to the next tier.

Should I measure the grout gap?

For most calculations, grout gap has a small effect — on a 12"×12" tile with a 1/8" joint, it adds about 1% to your area requirement. However, for large projects or smaller tiles (like 4"×4" mosaics), the grout gap becomes more significant and can affect your total by 3–5%. This calculator includes it for accuracy.

What if my room is not rectangular?

Break the room into rectangular sections and calculate each one separately, then add the totals together. Alternatively, measure the total bounding rectangle and subtract any areas that won't be tiled (like a built-in closet). Use the "Known Area" tab to enter your calculated square footage directly.

Can I use this for wall tiles too?

Absolutely. The calculation is identical — enter the wall height as your "length" and wall width as your "width." For a shower, calculate each wall section separately and add them up before entering the total in the Known Area tab. Remember that wall installations often have more cuts around fixtures, so use at least 15% waste.
