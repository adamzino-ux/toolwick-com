Why This Matters

Board feet is the standard unit of measure for hardwood lumber in North America — and getting it wrong can cost you real money. A hobbyist building a walnut dining table might need 40–60 board feet, while a contractor framing a small addition could need several hundred. At $5–$15 per board foot for hardwood, a miscalculation of just 10 board feet means $50–$150 wasted or a mid-project run to the lumber yard.

Unlike softwood lumber (sold by the linear foot), hardwood pricing is almost always quoted per board foot. Understanding the difference matters when comparing prices at different suppliers. This calculator also helps you translate rough-cut dimensional lumber (like a 2×4 at 1.5"×3.5" actual size) into accurate board-foot costs — many buyers forget to account for the difference between nominal and actual dimensions.

Whether you're a weekend woodworker, a cabinetmaker quoting a job, or a homeowner buying deck boards, knowing your exact board footage prevents over-buying and budget overruns.

How It's Calculated

A board foot is a unit of volume equal to a piece of wood 1 inch thick, 12 inches wide, and 12 inches long (144 cubic inches).

Board Feet = (Thickness" × Width" × Length') ÷ 12

For example: a board that is 1" thick, 6" wide, and 8 feet long = (1 × 6 × 8) ÷ 12 = 4 board feet.

For multiple quantities, multiply by the count. Total board feet for a project = sum of all individual board calculations. If you're working in metric, the calculator converts mm to inches and meters to feet before applying the formula.

Linear Feet = Length (ft) × Quantity  |  Cost = Total BF × Price/BF

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Nominal vs. actual size: A "2×4" is actually 1.5" × 3.5". Use actual dimensions for accurate board-foot calculations, not the nominal lumber name.
  • Add a waste factor: Always buy 10–15% extra to account for defects, cuts, and mistakes — especially with figured or knotty wood.
  • Rough vs. surfaced lumber: Rough-cut lumber is sold at its full stated thickness. S4S (surfaced four sides) lumber has been planed down. Always measure what you're actually buying.
  • Don't confuse board feet with square feet: Board feet account for thickness; square feet do not. Two ½" boards covering the same floor area as one 1" board = same square footage but same board footage too — but twice the pieces.
  • Price quotes: When comparing suppliers, always confirm whether prices are per board foot or per linear foot — they are not interchangeable for boards wider than 12".

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a board foot and how does it differ from a linear foot?

A board foot is a volume measurement (thickness × width × length) that accounts for the size of the wood in three dimensions. A linear foot is simply the length. A 1"×6" board that's 10 feet long is 10 linear feet but only 5 board feet. Hardwood is almost always sold by board feet; dimensional framing lumber is often sold by linear feet.

How do I calculate board feet for a 2×4?

Use the actual dimensions, not the nominal name. A 2×4 is actually 1.5 inches thick and 3.5 inches wide. So a 10-foot 2×4 = (1.5 × 3.5 × 10) ÷ 12 = 4.375 board feet. Many people mistakenly use 2" and 4" and end up overestimating by about 52%.

Can I use this calculator for metric measurements?

Yes — switch to "Metric" in the dropdown and enter thickness in millimeters, width in millimeters, and length in meters. The calculator converts to imperial units internally and then applies the board-foot formula, giving you results in both systems.

How much should I add for waste?

A standard rule of thumb is 10% for clear, straight-grained lumber on simple projects, and 15–20% for figured wood, live-edge slabs, or complex cuts. If you're working with very knotty or defective wood, add up to 25%. This calculator shows your net board footage — multiply by 1.10 to 1.20 to get your purchase quantity.
