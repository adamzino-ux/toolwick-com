Why This Matters

Board feet is the standard unit of measurement used when buying and selling hardwood lumber in North America. Unlike linear feet or square feet, board feet accounts for all three dimensions — meaning a thick, wide board costs more per linear foot than a thin, narrow one. Understanding this can save you real money.

For example, if you're building a walnut dining table and need 8 boards at 1" × 6" × 8', that's exactly 32 board feet. At $8/BF for black walnut, that's $256 in rough lumber — before waste. Most woodworkers add a 15–25% waste factor for jointing, planing, and cutting.

Contractors ordering framing lumber, deck builders calculating pressure-treated stock, cabinet makers sourcing hardwood slabs — all of them use board feet daily. Getting the number wrong means an extra trip to the lumber yard, or worse, buying twice as much as you need.

How It's Calculated

A board foot equals a piece of wood that is 1 inch thick, 12 inches wide, and 12 inches long — exactly 144 cubic inches.

Board Feet = (Thickness_in × Width_in × Length_in) ÷ 144

Or equivalently, if length is in feet:

Board Feet = (Thickness_in × Width_in × Length_ft) ÷ 12

For multiple boards, multiply the single-board result by quantity, then sum all entries. The diagram above updates with your actual measurements when you calculate.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Use nominal, not actual dimensions. A "2×4" is actually 1.5" × 3.5" after milling. Lumber yards charge for the nominal (rough-sawn) size, so use 2" × 4" when calculating board feet for purchasing.
  • Add a waste factor. For hardwood projects, add 15–20% to your board foot total for jointing, surfacing, and defect removal. For rough construction, 10% is usually enough.
  • Don't confuse board feet with linear feet. Home improvement stores price softwood in linear feet; hardwood dealers use board feet. Know which you're buying.
  • Round up when ordering. Lumber doesn't come in fractions. If you need 47.3 board feet, order 50 or check what standard lengths get you closest.
  • Check your units. A common error is mixing inches and feet in the same calculation — e.g., entering 96 for length when the formula expects feet. Use this tool's unit selector to avoid that mistake.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a board foot?

A board foot (BF or FBM) is a unit of lumber volume equal to 144 cubic inches — or a board 1" thick, 12" wide, and 12" long. It's the standard unit for pricing hardwood lumber in North America. A 1" × 6" × 8' board contains exactly 4 board feet.

How many board feet are in a 2×4×8?

Using nominal dimensions: (2 × 4 × 96) ÷ 144 = 5.33 board feet. If you're calculating actual (dressed) dimensions of 1.5" × 3.5" × 96", the result is 3.5 board feet. For purchasing purposes, use the nominal size since that's how mills price lumber.

Is board feet the same as square feet?

No. Square feet measures area (length × width), while board feet measures volume (thickness × width × length ÷ 144). A 1-inch-thick board has the same square footage as a 2-inch-thick board of the same face dimensions, but the thicker board has twice the board feet — and costs more.

How do I calculate board feet for irregular slabs or live edge wood?

For live edge slabs, measure the average width (narrow end + wide end ÷ 2), then use that as your width input. Most hardwood dealers will measure and mark the board feet directly on the slab, but you can cross-check using this tool with average width and actual length.
