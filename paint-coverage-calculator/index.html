How to Use This Paint Coverage Calculator

Choose your preferred input mode: Room Mode lets you enter a room's length, width, and ceiling height to automatically calculate wall and ceiling area. Surface Mode lets you add individual surfaces (accent walls, stairwells, trim panels) one by one. Custom Area Mode is for when you already know the square footage. Then set your paint type, number of coats, and waste buffer — hit Calculate to see exactly how many gallons you need.

Why This Matters

Running out of paint mid-project is a nightmare — especially if your exact color batch is unavailable later. Buying too much wastes money and creates disposal headaches. A precise paint estimate prevents both problems.

Consider a typical 12×14 ft bedroom with 9-ft ceilings and one door and window: that's roughly 476 sq ft of wall space. With two coats of standard latex at 400 sq ft/gallon, you need about 2.4 gallons — which means buying 3 one-gallon cans. Without calculating, most people either buy 2 (not enough) or 4 (overspending by $30–50).

Professional painters always calculate coverage rates first. Paint type matters too: thick elastomeric paints cover 200–250 sq ft/gallon, while thin stains can cover 600+ sq ft. A cheap paint requiring three coats often costs more than a premium paint needing only two.

How It's Calculated

The formula is straightforward:

Wall Area = 2 × (Length + Width) × Height

Ceiling Area = Length × Width (if painting)

Deductions = Doors (21 sq ft each) + Windows (15 sq ft each)

Net Area = Wall Area + Ceiling Area − Deductions

Paint Needed (gal) = (Net Area × Coats) ÷ Coverage Rate × (1 + Waste%)

Coverage rate is typically 350–450 sq ft per gallon for one coat. The waste factor accounts for edges, porous surfaces, roller nap absorption, and touch-ups.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't skip the waste buffer. Even pros add 10–15% — surfaces are never perfectly smooth, and cut-in edges use more paint than rolling.
  • Round up to whole cans. Paint stores can't reseal opened cans. Always buy full gallons; leftover paint stores well for touch-ups.
  • Dark-to-light transitions need primer. Going from red to white? Add a primer coat to your count, not just the finish color coats.
  • Textured or new drywall absorbs more. Drop your coverage estimate by 20–25% for unpainted drywall or heavy orange-peel texture.
  • Don't over-deduct for small openings. Trim and reveals around windows actually add paintable surface; the net deduction is smaller than you'd think.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does one gallon of paint cover?

One gallon of standard latex or acrylic paint covers approximately 350–400 square feet with a single coat on a smooth, previously painted surface. Rough textures, bare drywall, or very porous masonry can reduce coverage to 200–300 sq ft per gallon.

Should I calculate by room or by total area?

For a single room, Room Mode is fastest. For multi-room projects, add each room's area separately (to account for different colors) or sum them in Custom Area mode if using one color throughout. Keeping rooms separate lets you buy the right quantity per color.

How many coats of paint do I actually need?

Two coats is the standard for most painting projects and gives full color depth and durability. One coat may suffice for light colors over similar existing colors or with high-hide paints. Three or more coats are typically needed when switching from a very dark to a very light color, or painting bare new drywall.

What's included in the door and window deduction?

This calculator deducts 21 sq ft per standard interior door (3×7 ft) and 15 sq ft per standard window (3×5 ft). Large windows, sliding doors, or French doors are larger — for those, use Custom Area mode and manually subtract their dimensions.
