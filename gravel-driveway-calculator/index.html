How to Use This Gravel Driveway Calculator

Enter your driveway's length and width in feet, then use the slider to set your desired gravel depth in inches. Select the type of gravel you plan to use (each has a different weight per cubic yard), and optionally enter your price per ton and delivery fee. Click Calculate Gravel Needed to instantly see cubic yards, tons, cubic feet, and a total cost estimate.

The results update based on your exact inputs. You can adjust the depth slider and recalculate to compare how different depths affect the total material needed.

Why This Matters

Ordering too little gravel means a second delivery trip — which often costs as much as the first. Order too much and you're paying to haul away excess material or leaving it piled in your yard for years. Getting the number right matters both financially and practically.

A standard residential driveway that's 100 feet long and 12 feet wide at 4 inches deep needs about 14.8 cubic yards or roughly 22 tons of crushed granite — that's a significant purchase. At $45/ton plus $75 delivery, you're looking at nearly $1,065. A longer driveway at 200 feet jumps to about $2,055. These are real numbers that justify taking 60 seconds to calculate before you call your supplier.

This tool is useful for homeowners planning new driveways, contractors pricing jobs, landscapers replenishing coverage on existing gravel paths, and anyone expanding or regrading a driveway. Even if you're just getting a ballpark for a renovation budget, having a proper cubic yard and tonnage figure prevents the most common (and expensive) ordering mistake: guessing.

How It's Calculated

The calculation follows standard volumetric math used by gravel suppliers and contractors. Here's exactly what's happening:

Volume (ft³) = Length (ft) × Width (ft) × Depth (ft)
Depth (ft) = Depth (in) ÷ 12

Volume (yd³) = Volume (ft³) ÷ 27

Weight (tons) = Volume (yd³) × Tons per Cubic Yard

Total Cost = (Weight × Price per Ton) + Delivery Cost

There are 27 cubic feet in one cubic yard. Gravel weight varies by material: crushed granite runs about 1.5 tons/yd³, while pea gravel is lighter at ~1.4 tons/yd³ and river rock heavier at ~1.6 tons/yd³. These figures are industry averages — your supplier may quote slightly different densities depending on moisture content and specific rock source.

A standard recommendation is 4 inches of depth for a new driveway base layer. For a top dressing refresh, 2 inches is often sufficient. High-traffic or heavy-vehicle driveways benefit from 6 inches.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Add a 10% buffer. Always order slightly more than you calculate — gravel settles and compacts over time, and minor measurement errors are common. Most suppliers round to the nearest half-ton anyway.
  • Measure carefully. If your driveway isn't a rectangle, break it into sections. Calculate each rectangle separately and add the totals together before ordering.
  • Don't confuse cubic yards with tons. Suppliers quote prices in tons; your calculation starts in cubic yards. Use the density factor for your specific gravel type to convert accurately.
  • Check minimum delivery loads. Many gravel suppliers have a minimum delivery of 10 or 15 tons. If your project is small, a pickup truckload from a landscape supply yard may be more economical.
  • Base vs. top layer. A well-built driveway often has two layers: a coarse base (crushed stone #3 or #4) topped with a finer finishing gravel (pea gravel or #57 stone). Calculate each layer separately using this tool.

Frequently Asked Questions

How deep should my gravel driveway be?

For a new driveway, the industry-standard recommendation is 4 inches of compacted gravel as a base, often with an additional 2-inch top layer of finer material. If you're just topping off an existing gravel driveway that's gotten thin, 1–2 inches is usually sufficient. Driveways that handle heavy trucks or frequent traffic benefit from a 6-inch base.

What type of gravel is best for driveways?

Crushed stone (#57 or #411) is the most common choice for driveways because angular edges lock together and compact well. Pea gravel looks attractive but tends to scatter and doesn't compact as firmly. Decomposed granite is popular in dry climates and binds well when wet. Your local supplier will know what material holds up best in your region.

How many tons are in a cubic yard of gravel?

It depends on the gravel type, but most crushed stone and gravel weighs between 1.35 and 1.6 tons per cubic yard. A commonly used average is 1.5 tons per cubic yard for general estimation. Always confirm the specific density with your supplier before placing a large order, since moisture content can shift the weight.

Can I use this for a circular or curved driveway?

This calculator handles rectangular shapes directly. For a circular turnaround, calculate the area using the formula: Area = π × radius². For irregular curves, break the driveway into straight rectangular sections, calculate each, and add the totals. A landscape professional can also measure irregular shapes with a measuring wheel for precision.
