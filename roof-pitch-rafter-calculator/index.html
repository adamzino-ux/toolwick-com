Why This Matters

Roof pitch affects everything from material costs to structural safety to local building code compliance. A homeowner adding a shed roof, a contractor bidding a re-roof job, or a DIYer framing a garage — everyone needs to know pitch before they can order materials or pull a permit.

For example, a 6:12 pitch (26.6°) is a very common residential choice — steep enough to shed rain and snow effectively, but not so steep that it makes roofing dangerous. A 4:12 pitch works well for asphalt shingles but is right at the lower limit. Drop below 3:12 and you typically need special low-slope roofing materials. Go above 8:12 and material waste increases because sheets must be cut at sharper angles, and safety harnesses become mandatory on most job sites.

Rafter length is equally critical. Ordering lumber 6 inches too short means a trip back to the lumber yard. Ordering 2 feet too long wastes money on every single rafter — on a 30-foot house with rafters 16" on center, that's over 22 rafters per side, meaning serious cost overruns on a simple mistake.

How It's Calculated

Roof pitch is expressed as a ratio of rise to run, always over 12 inches of run. Angle and rafter length use the Pythagorean theorem:

Pitch Ratio = Rise / Run × 12 (normalized to X:12) Roof Angle = arctan(Rise / Run) in degrees Rafter Length (per foot of run) = √(Rise² + Run²) Full Rafter Length = √(Rise² + Half-Span²) [when span is provided] With Overhang = Full Rafter Length + √(Rise_per_in² + Overhang²)

The rafter length per foot of run is the multiplier (also called the rafter factor) — multiply it by your actual run in feet to get total rafter length. When you enter a full span, the calculator uses half that span as the run automatically.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Rise is always measured per 12" of run — a 6:12 pitch means 6 inches of rise for every 12 inches of horizontal run, not per foot of rafter.
  • Don't confuse span with run. Span is the full width of the building; run is half the span (for a symmetric gable). This calculator handles both.
  • Add at least 10% waste factor when ordering lumber. Cuts, defects, and measurement errors are always present on real job sites.
  • Overhang matters more than you think. A 12-inch overhang on a steep roof can add 14+ inches to rafter length — that's the difference between a 10-foot and a 12-foot board.
  • Check local codes before finalizing pitch. Many jurisdictions have minimum pitch requirements for specific roofing materials — metal roofing, for instance, sometimes needs only 1:12, while standard 3-tab shingles typically require at least 4:12.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a "standard" roof pitch?

Most residential homes in North America use a pitch between 4:12 and 9:12. The most common single pitch is 6:12 (26.6°), which balances aesthetics, weather performance, and ease of installation. Low-slope commercial roofs are typically under 3:12, while steep decorative roofs can be 12:12 or higher.

What's the difference between roof pitch and roof slope?

They describe the same thing in different ways. Pitch is expressed as a ratio (6:12), while slope is often expressed as a percentage or decimal (0.5 or 50%). Slope = Rise ÷ Run. In roofing, "pitch" is the dominant term in the US and Canada, while "slope" is more common in engineering and architecture contexts.

Does rafter length include the ridge board or wall plate?

This calculator gives the structural rafter length from the ridge centerline to the top of the wall plate, plus any overhang you specify. It does not account for ridge board deduction (typically half the ridge board thickness, often ¾"–1½") or bird's mouth cuts. Add those deductions manually when cutting actual lumber.

Can I use this for flat or low-slope roofs?

Yes, though pitch below 1:12 is typically classified as a "flat roof" and requires membrane or built-up roofing systems rather than shingles. The calculator works for any positive rise value. Enter 1 rise and 12 run for a 1:12 pitch. Note that a truly flat roof (0:12) would have infinite potential for water pooling and is generally avoided in construction.
