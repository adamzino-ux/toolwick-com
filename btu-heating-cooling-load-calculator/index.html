Why This Matters

Choosing the wrong size HVAC unit is one of the most expensive mistakes homeowners make. An undersized 1.5-ton unit trying to cool a 600 sq ft room in Phoenix will run constantly, jack up electricity bills, and fail prematurely. An oversized 5-ton system in a 900 sq ft Minneapolis apartment will short-cycle — cooling air so fast the system never dehumidifies properly, leaving you cold but clammy.

Industry rule of thumb gives you 20 BTU per square foot — but that's wildly inaccurate for real-world conditions. A south-facing Florida sunroom needs 50+ BTU/sq ft. A well-insulated Minnesota bedroom might need only 15 BTU/sq ft for cooling but 40 BTU/sq ft for heating. This calculator adjusts for climate zone (heating degree days), insulation quality, solar gain from sun exposure, occupant heat load (each person generates ~250 BTU/hr at rest), and kitchen appliance loads.

HVAC contractors use Manual J load calculations for this exact purpose. This tool gives you a solid ballpark before calling a contractor — so you walk in knowing whether that 3-ton quote makes sense or if you're being upsold.

How It's Calculated

The base load starts with floor area and ceiling height to get room volume, then applies BTU-per-square-foot factors adjusted by climate and insulation:

Base Cooling Load = Floor Area × Base BTU/sq ft × Insulation Factor × Climate Factor

Window Load = Number of Windows × Avg Window Area × Solar Heat Gain × Sun Factor

Occupant Load = Occupants × 250 BTU/hr

Total Cooling BTU = Base + Window + Occupant + Room-Type Appliance Load

Heating load uses a similar method but with heating degree-day factors. Climate multipliers range from 0.7× (hot climates need less heating) to 1.6× (very cold climates). Insulation multipliers range from 1.3× (poor) to 0.75× (excellent). Tonnage is derived by dividing BTU/hr by 12,000 (1 ton = 12,000 BTU/hr).

These values align with ASHRAE residential guidelines and Manual J simplified methods for preliminary sizing.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Don't ignore insulation: Upgrading from poor to good insulation can cut your BTU requirement by 35–40%, saving hundreds per year in energy costs.
  • Account for climate: The same 400 sq ft room needs ~8,000 BTU cooling in San Francisco but ~14,000 BTU in Houston.
  • Kitchens run hot: Stoves, ovens, and refrigerators add 1,000–4,000 BTU/hr of heat. Always size kitchen AC higher than living areas of equal square footage.
  • Don't forget basement conditions: Basements often have stable temperatures. Oversizing a basement unit leads to moisture problems from short-cycling.
  • Always get a Manual J: For new construction or full system replacement, ask your HVAC contractor for a full Manual J load calculation — this tool gives you a solid estimate, not a certified engineering report.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does BTU/hr actually mean?

BTU stands for British Thermal Unit — the energy required to raise 1 pound of water by 1°F. In HVAC, BTU/hr (often shortened to BTU) measures how fast a heating or cooling system adds or removes heat. A 12,000 BTU/hr air conditioner removes 12,000 BTUs of heat energy from your space every hour.

How many BTUs per square foot is standard?

A commonly cited figure is 20 BTU per square foot for cooling — so a 500 sq ft room would need a 10,000 BTU unit. However, this ignores climate, ceiling height, windows, and insulation. In a hot, sunny climate with poor insulation, you might need 35–45 BTU/sq ft. In a cool climate with excellent insulation, 12–15 BTU/sq ft may suffice.

What size AC unit do I need for 1,000 sq ft?

At the 20 BTU/sq ft baseline, a 1,000 sq ft space needs roughly 20,000 BTU — that's a 1.5-ton to 2-ton unit. But adjust for your climate: in Phoenix or Miami, lean toward 2.5 tons; in Seattle or Denver with good insulation, 1.5 tons may be sufficient. This calculator will give you a personalized number.

Is a bigger HVAC unit always better?

No — this is a common and costly mistake. Oversized units short-cycle: they blast cold air quickly, then shut off before completing a full humidity-removal cycle. This leaves rooms feeling clammy and humid even at the right temperature. It also stresses components, reducing equipment lifespan. Proper sizing is critical for both comfort and efficiency.
